What I work on

In simple words: I study how biological and robotic agents predict and synchronize with rhythmic events. Rhythm turns out to be a surprisingly complex problem for the majority of animal species other than humans and parrots 🦜! To “feel the beat” and “anticipate the bass drop”, which comes so naturally to us on a random Friday night out, there is in fact a lot of going on.

Rhythm sits at the intersection of perception, action, prediction, and social coordination. Whether you are tapping along to music or switching turns talking to someone, your brain is doing something computationally fascinating. And through my research, I want to understand what that is.

My research spans several areas: I build computational models of timing and synchronization, run behavioral experiments on rhythm perception and synchronization, and design multi-agent platforms that let me test theories and visualize collective coordination, both in silico and with physical robots.

Why rhythm?

Rhythm is predictive. Contrary to common intuition, you don't just react to beats, you anticipate them. This anticipatory quality is intricate in time, and understanding it sheds light on language, music, movement, and social bonding. In my eyes, there's something almost magical about how a group of people can spontaneously lock into a shared pulse without anyone commanding them to do so. I find it naturally intriguing to ask: what makes this possible?

The robot angle

One of my favorite ways to put cognitive theory to test is to try to reconstruct it in silico. My tools? Robots and agent-based simulations: if I give a robot my model of rhythmic prediction, can it synchronize predictively and robustly? What breaks?

What does that tell me about what the brain must be doing? Can I have a group of agents move in time-locked manner? Can I push further and apply theoretical ideas in real life? These are the questions I love to ask.

SYNCOFLY: Collective Rhythm Synchronization Platform

A browser-based multi-agent simulation sandbox for studying how groups stay in sync under disruption

▾
■ system message
_
□
✕

The big question: When rhythm signal or partial group agents gets disrupted, what enables the group as a whole retain structural stability? How do reactive followers and predictive agents differ in their behaviors?

SYNCOFLY is a browser-based platform I built to study collective rhythmic synchronization under perturbation. Five simulated agents orbit a rhythmic source and must synchronize to it while influencing one another. Each agent can be configured as predictive (internally maintaining a sparse updating hierarchical representation) or reactive (responding reactively to incoming events).

The platform supports non-isochronous rhythms, beat omission, pattern switching, noise injection, and real-time behavioral logging. All configurable for users without coding requirement!

Key questions

?How does predictive timing capacity distributed across a group affect collective resilience?
?Can reactive agents inherit temporal structure from predictive neighbors via social coupling?
?When does social coupling stabilize a group, and when does it propagate noise?

What I found

Predictive agents maintained stable synchronization even when 30% of beats were randomly dropped or the meter switched mid-run. Peer-dominant reactive agents could inherit the predictive agents' inferred temporal structure through social coupling, including beats that were never actually played. But stronger coupling was a double-edged sword: when the predictive neighbor became noisy, the reactive agent propagated that noise too.

Try SYNCOFLY live

Runs entirely in the browser — no installation needed. Configure agents, inject perturbations, and watch synchronization unfold.

Rhythmic Coordinating Robots

Does having an internal beat actually help robots stay in sync?

▾
■ system message
_
□
✕

The inspiration: I want to implement internal rhythmic models on robots! Does current prominent theory on predictive model still hold in the real world where unpredictable noises are perpetuating the system both externally and internally?

This pilot project is the embodied precursor of SYNCOFLY. I used e-puck2 robots to investigate the functional role of an internal tempo in rhythmic coordination. Rather than just asking "can robots tap along to a beat?", I asked: can I replicate predictive rhythm synchronization behavior on robots?

This project was presented and demoed to the UCI Cognitive Sciences department as my Third-year talk milestone.

Key questions

?Can physical robots embodying an internal tempo stay in sync?
?What does the robotic case reveal about humans' intricate internal tempo system?

The Role of Syllable Position in English Lexical Stress

Do nonnative English speakers produce stress differently? Why?

▾
■ system message
_
□
✕

The question: English stress is marked mainly by making unstressed syllables shorter. But does it matter whether that syllable comes first or last in the word? And do Mandarin learners of English use the same strategies as native speakers?

English has pairs of words where stress alone distinguishes nouns from verbs: REcord (noun) vs. reCORD (verb). Native speakers mark stress with a distinctive pattern of duration, pitch, and intensity. Importantly, the pattern isn't identical across word positions.

In a study that samples native American English speakers and L1-Mandarin English learners, we found a positional asymmetry in how speakers use different cues, and that unstressed syllables are reduced more aggressively in initial position than in final position.

This work is collected, analyzed, and is currently under manuscript writing process.

Key questions

?Do native English speakers show position-specific strategies for stress marking?
?Do L1 Mandarin speakers transfer their pitch-control strategies into English stress production?
?What does this tell us about how experience shapes the phonological system in speech?

Self-Monitoring through Rhythm: Prosodic Planning & Delayed Auditory Feedback

What's really disrupted when you hear your own voice late?

▾
■ system message
_
□
✕

The setup: Delayed auditory feedback (DAF) disrupts speech, most severely at about 200ms delay. But is it disrupting the sounds you make, or the timing you planned?

When you hear your own voice played back with a short delay, your speech can become disfluent, slow down, or simply break down. This project directly tests whether prosodic frames are the target of DAF disruption. We compare natural prosodic speech against monotone speech across three delay conditions.

Key questions

?Is DAF disruption primarily phonetic/segmental or prosodic/suprasegmental?
?Does removing prosodic rhythm reduce DAF-induced disfluency?

Rhythm perception Motor synchronization SMPC 2026

When Perception Outperforms Action: Dissociating Rhythm Perception from Motor Synchronization

Can you perceive a rhythm without being able to tap it?

▾
■ system message
_
□
✕

The question: Many theories assume rhythm perception depends on motor simulation, which means that to perceive rhythm, you must also be able to synchronize with it. But is that actually true?

Using polyrhythmic stimuli (3:5, 3:7, 5:7) in a within-subjects design, I compared how well participants could detect timing irregularities (perception) versus tap simultaneously to both streams (synchronization).

Nearly everyone achieved high perceptual sensitivity, but many of the same people failed to synchronize even under generous criteria. This demonstrates that, at the bottom line, perceptual rhythm representations can be more robust and accurate than what is directly measurable in motor execution.

Key questions

?Rethink: Is motor synchronization actually equivalent to or an adequate measurement for rhythm perception, or are these dissociable?
?Does the perception-action gap reflect motor execution limits or higher-level processing differences?
